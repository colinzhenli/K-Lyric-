K-Lyric

Keyword-Conditioned Chinese Lyric Generation

Eric Wang, Zhen Li
2 / 10

Problem

How can we generate fluent, creative lyrics that are guaranteed to contain user-specified keywords?

Why It Matters

  • Songwriters often start from a few key concepts and need help expanding them into full lyric lines
  • Lexically constrained generation is a fundamental NLP challenge — ensuring specific tokens appear in output while maintaining fluency
  • Applicable beyond lyrics: poetry, slogans, story writing with plot constraints

The Core Tension

Fluency — output should read like natural, coherent Chinese

vs.

Keyword Fidelity — every user-specified keyword must appear in the output

Standard language models optimize for fluency but provide no guarantee about keyword inclusion. Forcing keywords in often breaks fluency. We need a method that structurally guarantees both.

3 / 10

Current Approaches & Limitations

Unconstrained LMs (GPT-2, etc.)

+ Generates fluent text

− No guarantee keywords appear in output

− Must sample repeatedly and hope for inclusion

Constrained Beam Search (Hokamp & Liu, 2017)

+ Guarantees keyword presence

− Hurts fluency by forcing tokens at arbitrary positions

− Exponential complexity with more constraints

BERT-style Infilling (Donahue et al., 2020)

+ Can fill around fixed tokens

− Fixed output length (one [MASK] = one token)

− Not designed for variable-length generation

Template-based Methods

+ Full control over structure

− Rigid, unnatural phrasing

− Requires hand-crafted templates per pattern

Our Insight: Frame keyword-conditioned generation as span infilling — fix keywords in place and let the model fill in variable-length text around them. This naturally aligns with T5's pre-training objective (span corruption), giving us both keyword guarantees and fluency.

4 / 10

Approach

The encoder reads keyword-structured input; the decoder generates text segments to fill around the keywords; post-processing merges them into the final lyric.

Results

No single ground truth for open-ended generation → evaluate fluency via Perplexity (PPL) from an independent Chinese GPT-2 (uer/gpt2-chinese-cluecorpussmall). Lower PPL = more fluent.

Analysis

Successful Generations

Keywords → Generated Lyric   PPL

take away, smile → "Took away your smile" (43)

time, butterfly → "Time flies like a butterfly" (26)

hope, have → "Hope you can have me" (24)

atmosphere → "Let this atmosphere become gentler" (54)

Failure Cases

circle → "circle circle circle..." (2.6)

lyricist, Jian Ling → "Lyricist Jian Ling Zheng Bingbing" (3078)

Failure Patterns

  • Repetition: model repeats keyword endlessly (low PPL but meaningless)
  • Rare vocabulary: archaic words yield incoherent output
  • Metadata leakage: generates artist names from training noise

Fluency vs. Creativity: Our model scores lower PPL than real lyrics (54 vs 136). This reveals a tradeoff — the model favors safe, common phrasings, while real songwriters use unusual poetic combinations that GPT-2 rates as less predictable. PPL measures fluency, not quality.

9 / 10

Conclusions

What We Showed

  • 100% keyword fidelity by construction
  • Median PPL 54 vs. 136 for real lyrics — strong fluency
  • Zero human annotation — fully automatic data pipeline

Limitations

  • Repetition degeneration on common keywords
  • Poor results on rare / classical vocabulary
  • Training stopped early (~30 epochs); loss still decreasing
  • No human evaluation — only automatic PPL

Future Work

  • Scale to larger mT5 variants (base, large)
  • Contrastive decoding to reduce repetition
  • Multi-line generation (full verses)
  • Human evaluation on fluency, creativity, relevance
  • Compare against constrained decoding baselines
